## A Simple Hangboard Decision Framework

Do not start with the product. Start with the training question.

### 1. Is finger strength actually the limiter?

If you are falling because your feet cut, your hips are out of position, or you cannot read sequences, hangboard training may not be the best first fix.

Use [How to Read Climbing Routes Before You Climb](/blog/how-to-read-climbing-routes-before-you-climb/) and [Climbing Gym Workout Plan](/blog/climbing-gym-workout-plan/) to clean up the climbing side first.

### 2. Can you recover from extra finger work?

Finger training is high signal when placed well and high risk when stacked on top of too much hard climbing.

The [CDC physical activity guidelines](https://www.cdc.gov/physical-activity-basics/guidelines/adults.html) recommend adults include muscle-strengthening activity as part of a balanced week. Climbers still need to place finger-specific loading carefully because it is more localized and intense than general strength work.

### 3. Will measurement change your decisions?

Climbro is most useful when measurement affects behavior.

That could mean you reduce intensity after a poor test, repeat a training block because it worked, or stop guessing whether a session was productive.

If data will not change your decisions, a simpler setup may be enough.

### 4. Do you want ownership or a lower-commitment path?

Unsure buyers should not force an ownership decision. Climbro publishes rental and 30-day trial paths, and those serve different kinds of uncertainty.

Rental fits behavior uncertainty: "Will I actually use this?"

The 30-day trial path fits buyer reassurance: "I already expect to buy new, but I want a policy path around the purchase."

Compare both in [Climbro Rental vs 30-Day Trial](/blog/climbro-rental-vs-30-day-trial/).

## How Climbro Fits Into A Normal Training Week

A simple structure might look like this:

| Day | Focus |
|---|---|
| Monday | Technique climbing or easy volume |
| Tuesday | Rest or general strength |
| Wednesday | Hard climbing or projecting |
| Thursday | Rest |
| Friday | Finger session or guided hangboard work |
| Saturday | Moderate climbing |
| Sunday | Rest, mobility, or easy movement |

That is only a template. The important principle is separation: avoid stacking max finger loading directly on top of the hardest climbing days unless you already know you recover well.

If endurance is the bigger limiter, use [Climbing Endurance Training](/blog/climbing-endurance-training-indoor-climbers/) or [Climbing Power Endurance Training](/blog/climbing-power-endurance-training/) before making finger work the center of the plan.

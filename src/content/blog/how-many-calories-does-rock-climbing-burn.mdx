Rock climbing can burn a lot of calories, but the exact number is easy to overstate.

The reason is simple: climbing sessions include rest. A hard bouldering attempt may be intense for 20 seconds, followed by two or three minutes of standing, brushing holds, talking, or waiting. A top-rope route may last several minutes, but you still spend part of the session belaying or resting.

So the honest answer is a range, not one magic number.

## Quick Answer

Indoor rock climbing commonly burns about 300 to 700 calories in a 90-minute session, depending on body weight, climbing style, route intensity, rest time, and whether you are climbing, belaying, or training between attempts. Bouldering often feels more intense per attempt, while top rope and lead climbing can create more continuous work if you climb many routes.

## The Simple Formula

The standard exercise estimate uses METs, short for metabolic equivalents. The formula is:

```
Calories burned = MET x body weight in kg x duration in hours
```

The <a href="https://pacompendium.com/sports/" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">Compendium of Physical Activities</a> lists rock or mountain climbing activities with MET values that vary by effort and context. Indoor gym sessions are not identical to outdoor climbing, but METs give a better starting point than guessing from a smartwatch alone.

For practical indoor estimates, use these rough ranges:

| Session type | Practical MET estimate | What it usually means |
|---|---:|---|
| Easy beginner session | 4 to 5 | Learning movement, lots of rest |
| Moderate gym climbing | 5 to 7 | Steady climbing with normal breaks |
| Hard bouldering or lead intervals | 7 to 9 | Intense attempts, shorter rests |

## Calories Burned by Body Weight

Here are practical estimates for a 60-minute active climbing window. Real 90-minute sessions often include less than 60 minutes of true active climbing, so use this table as a working range rather than a promise.

| Body weight | Moderate climbing, 5 MET | Hard climbing, 8 MET |
|---:|---:|---:|
| 125 lb | 284 cal/hour | 454 cal/hour |
| 150 lb | 341 cal/hour | 545 cal/hour |
| 175 lb | 397 cal/hour | 635 cal/hour |
| 200 lb | 454 cal/hour | 726 cal/hour |

If that looks lower than a fitness tracker estimate, rest time is usually the reason.

## Why Climbing Calorie Estimates Vary So Much

### Rest time changes everything

Two climbers can spend 90 minutes in the same bouldering gym and have completely different energy demands.

One climbs 20 problems with long rests. The other repeats easier climbs continuously, adds traversing, and finishes with core work. Same gym. Same time block. Different calorie burn.

This is why our <a href="/tools/training-load-recovery-planner/">Training Load & Recovery Planner</a> focuses on session stress, not just minutes spent inside the building.

### Bouldering is spiky

Bouldering can feel brutal because individual attempts are powerful. But hard attempts are short. If you rest properly, the total calorie burn may be moderate even though your forearms feel destroyed.

For new climbers, our <a href="/blog/what-is-bouldering/">bouldering guide</a> explains why short walls can still feel physically demanding.

### Rope climbing can be more continuous

Top rope and lead climbing often involve longer time on the wall. A route may include sustained pulling, footwork, clipping, resting positions, and controlled lowering.

But if you climb with one partner, half the session may be belaying. Belaying is active and attentive, but it is not the same workload as climbing.

### Skill level matters

Beginners often burn energy inefficiently. They overgrip, pull too much with the arms, and pause in poor positions. Experienced climbers may move with less wasted effort, but they also climb harder routes.

This is why technique can change both performance and fatigue. Read <a href="/blog/how-to-improve-climbing-technique/">How to Improve Climbing Technique</a> if your sessions feel exhausting before your muscles are actually trained.

## Is Climbing Good for Weight Loss?

Climbing can support weight loss, but it works best as part of a consistent weekly routine rather than as a calorie-burning hack.

The <a href="https://health.gov/our-work/nutrition-physical-activity/physical-activity-guidelines" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">U.S. Physical Activity Guidelines</a> recommend regular aerobic activity plus muscle-strengthening activity for adults. Climbing can contribute to both depending on session structure, but it does not replace sleep, nutrition, or total weekly movement.

The sustainable path is boring but effective:

- Climb two to three times per week
- Keep most sessions submaximal
- Add walking or easy cardio on non-climbing days
- Strength train weak links carefully
- Eat enough protein and avoid crash dieting

## How to Burn More Calories Without Wrecking Your Climbing

Do not turn every session into frantic circuit training. That usually makes your technique worse and your fingers angrier.

Instead, use these safer levers.

### Warm up longer

A 15-minute warm-up adds useful movement volume and reduces the urge to jump straight onto hard problems. Easy traversing, mobility, and very easy climbs all count.

### Repeat easy routes with better movement

Repeating easy climbs with silent feet, straight arms, and smooth hips builds skill and volume at the same time. It is better than flailing on one hard problem for an hour.

### Reduce phone breaks

You do not need short rests on every climb. You need appropriate rests. If half your session disappears into scrolling, your calorie estimate will be inflated.

### Add low-risk finishers

After climbing, add 8 to 12 minutes of core, mobility, or antagonist work. Keep it controlled. The goal is better fitness, not leaving too tired to recover.

## What About Fitness Trackers?

Smartwatches can be helpful for trends, but indoor climbing is hard for wrist-based trackers. Your wrist may flex, press against holds, get covered by chalk, or lose optical contact during hard movements.

Use your tracker to compare your own sessions over time. Do not treat one calorie number as precise.

Better session notes include:

- Number of climbs attempted
- Number of climbs completed
- Max grade attempted
- How long you rested
- How recovered you feel the next day

## FAQ

### Does bouldering burn more calories than top rope?

Not always. Bouldering is usually more intense per attempt, but top rope can create more continuous time on the wall. Total calorie burn depends on volume and rest.

### How many calories does 30 minutes of rock climbing burn?

For many adults, 30 minutes of active moderate climbing lands around 140 to 230 calories. Harder climbing or heavier body weight can push that higher.

### Is climbing better than running for calories?

Running is usually easier to measure and can burn more calories continuously. Climbing adds skill, strength, grip, mobility, and problem-solving, so the better choice depends on your goal.

### Does belaying burn calories?

Yes, but less than climbing. Belaying still requires standing, rope management, attention, and occasional movement, but it is not equivalent to being on the wall.

## Quick Answer

Based on Climbro's official site checked on **March 15, 2026**, Climbro says it measures key parts of finger and forearm performance, then converts those results into climbing-style outputs that help users identify strengths, weaknesses, and training priorities.

The clearest live methodology page says Climbro's **Performance Test Battery** is made up of four tests:

- **Max Strength**
- **Continuous**
- **Intermittent**
- **Finger Hang**

Climbro's public pages also reference measurement categories such as:

- maximal finger strength
- endurance
- strength endurance
- power

And Climbro's detailed results pages list metrics such as:

- hang time
- max force
- average force
- time in target zone
- force-time based performance indicators

The short version is this:

Climbro is not only trying to tell you whether you are "strong." It is trying to separate different parts of finger and forearm performance, then use that information to guide training.

## Why Climbro Starts With Testing

Climbro's official positioning is not "buy the board and do random hangs."

The live site repeatedly frames Climbro as a process:

1. test
2. identify weak points
3. generate a personal training plan
4. follow the plan
5. retest

That matters because it changes what Climbro is actually selling.

If a normal hangboard mostly sells a training surface, Climbro sells a training surface plus a decision system.

The `How it works` page is especially clear on this point. It says the first thing to do with Climbro is execute the **Performance Test Battery**, then use those results to get a personal training plan aimed at your weakest point.

That means the testing layer is not a side feature. It is central to the product's official workflow.

## Max Strength

This is the easiest Climbro test name to understand and one of the most important ones in the official workflow.

Climbro's live pages say Max Strength is part of the testing system, and the `How it works` page also says users should perform the **Max Strength** test regularly because its results are used to define the absolute intensity of other tests and exercises.

That makes Max Strength more than just another score.

Inside Climbro's own system, it appears to function as a calibration point.

The `Test results` section on `How it works` also says results can include:

- absolute max force
- relative max force

So at minimum, Climbro clearly positions Max Strength as a foundational measurement that affects how the rest of the platform sets intensity.

## Continuous

On the current `How it works` page, **Continuous** is one of the four named battery tests.

The same page says the battery assesses finger strength and endurance, and its result categories include measures tied to work over time rather than a single peak effort.

The workout and result descriptions also refer to metrics such as:

- time in target zone
- average force
- performance over work duration

That makes Continuous one of the tests Climbro uses to understand sustained output rather than only maximal pulling force.

If you want the plain-English version, Continuous appears to sit on the endurance side of the system.

## Intermittent

**Intermittent** is also listed on the current `How it works` page as part of the Performance Test Battery.

Like Continuous, it fits the official idea that Climbro is assessing more than one type of finger performance.

Climbro's public pages do not fully unpack the underlying logic in the kind of detail a sport scientist might want, but the point of the test inside the product is clear enough:

it helps Climbro distinguish between different endurance-related or repeat-effort abilities rather than treating all finger capacity as one thing.

That is important because it is one of the biggest ways Climbro differentiates itself from a standard hangboard workflow.

A normal hangboard lets you choose a protocol.

Climbro is trying to tell you which kind of protocol you may need.

## Finger Hang

That gives this test one of the clearest measurement hooks on the public site.

Finger Hang is also one of the points where the site's terminology seems to have shifted over time, because older internal notes and some other current live references mention **Finger RFD** instead.

## How Climbro Uses Those Results

The official Climbro workflow after testing is straightforward.

The `How it works` page says:

- complete the battery
- get a personal training plan
- follow `16-24` training sessions
- retest afterward
- generate a new plan if needed

The page also says the battery results are valid for **one month**.

That matters because it shows Climbro does not position testing as a one-time curiosity. It positions testing as the front end of an ongoing training loop.

That loop is one of Climbro's strongest official differentiators.

## FAQ

### What are the Climbro battery tests?

As checked on March 15, 2026, Climbro's `How it works` page lists four battery tests: Max Strength, Continuous, Intermittent, and Finger Hang. Other live Climbro pages also reference Finger RFD in the advanced testing layer, so the public documentation is not perfectly consistent.

### Does Climbro only measure max strength?

No. Climbro's official site presents the system as measuring multiple parts of finger and forearm performance, not just maximal strength. Its public pages refer to endurance, strength endurance, power, and other result indicators such as average force, max force, time in target zone, and hang time.

### How long do Climbro results stay valid?

Climbro's `How it works` page says Performance Test Battery results stay valid for one month. After that, users need a new battery if they want a fresh personal training plan based on current fitness.

### Do you need premium to use the Performance Test Battery?

Based on Climbro's live FAQ and app-plan pages checked on March 15, 2026, yes. Climbro says the free app includes core hangboard features and Max-Strength Tests, while the full Performance Test Battery and Personal Training Plan are premium features.

### What happens after the tests?

According to Climbro's official workflow, users complete the battery, get a personal training plan, follow a 16-24 session plan in the app, and then retest to compare progress and generate the next plan.
